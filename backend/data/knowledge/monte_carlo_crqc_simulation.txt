Monte Carlo CRQC Arrival Simulation — QuShield Knowledge Base

OVERVIEW
========
QuShield uses Monte Carlo simulation to model the probabilistic arrival of a
Cryptographically Relevant Quantum Computer (CRQC) — the milestone at which RSA,
ECC, and Diffie-Hellman become computationally breakable.

Unlike simpler 3-scenario models (pessimistic/median/optimistic), Monte Carlo
provides a full probability distribution, allowing organisations to quantify risk
exposure at any confidence level.

DEFAULT PARAMETERS
==================
- Mode year (most likely CRQC arrival): 2032
- Sigma (distribution spread): 3.5 years
- Minimum year (physical impossibility before): 2027
- Maximum year (planning horizon): 2045
- Simulations: 10,000 samples

These defaults reflect expert consensus from the Global Risk Institute 2023 survey
and align with NIST's post-quantum transition timeline (NIST IR 8547).

LOG-NORMAL DISTRIBUTION
========================
CRQC arrival is modelled as a log-normal distribution for two key reasons:

1. RIGHT TAIL: Quantum computing breakthroughs are uncertain — delays are more
   probable than early arrivals. Log-normal naturally captures this asymmetry.

2. LOWER BOUND: The distribution has a hard floor at min_year (2027), reflecting
   the physical and engineering constraints of building a fault-tolerant quantum
   computer.

The underlying normal distribution parameters are:
  mu = ln(mode_year - min_year)
  sigma_ln = sigma / (mode_year - min_year)

Samples are drawn from lognormal(mu, sigma_ln) and shifted by min_year.

PERCENTILE INTERPRETATION
==========================
- P5  (Aggressive scenario): 5% probability CRQC arrives by this year.
  Use this for high-sensitivity data (SWIFT, core banking, nuclear).
  Typically ~2029-2030.

- P50 (Median scenario):    50% probability CRQC arrives by this year.
  Use as the central planning assumption for most assets.
  Typically ~2031-2033.

- P95 (Conservative scenario): 95% probability CRQC arrives by this year.
  Use for long-lived data archives and regulatory compliance timelines.
  Typically ~2038-2042.

MOSCA'S INEQUALITY — PROBABILISTIC EXTENSION
=============================================
Classical Mosca's theorem: X + Y > Z → IMMEDIATE ACTION REQUIRED

Where:
  X = migration time (years to implement PQC)
  Y = data shelf life (years data must remain confidential)
  Z = years until CRQC arrival

Monte Carlo extends this: instead of a single Z value, we sample 10,000 Z values
from the CRQC arrival distribution. For each simulation:
  exposed_i = (X + Y) > Z_i

Exposure probability = sum(exposed_i) / 10,000

This gives a continuous probability of quantum exposure per asset, rather than
a binary yes/no answer.

CERTIFICATE EXPIRY vs CRQC RACE
================================
Each TLS/X.509 certificate is classified into one of three states:

1. SAFE (PQC): Certificate already uses post-quantum algorithms (ML-KEM, ML-DSA,
   SLH-DSA). No action required.

2. NATURAL ROTATION: Certificate will expire before the CRQC median arrival year.
   The certificate can be replaced during its natural renewal cycle with a PQC
   algorithm. Low urgency.

3. AT RISK: Certificate will still be active when CRQC arrives (P50 estimate).
   Requires OUT-OF-BAND proactive replacement — cannot wait for natural expiry.
   These certificates are highest priority for PQC migration.

The classification uses the median arrival year as the threshold. Organisations
handling highly sensitive data should use P5 as a more conservative threshold.

PORTFOLIO SIMULATION
====================
For a portfolio of assets, QuShield runs a single set of 10,000 CRQC arrival
samples (same quantum breakthrough affects all assets simultaneously). This
correctly models the correlated nature of quantum risk — CRQC is a systemic
event, not asset-specific.

Portfolio metrics:
- avg_assets_exposed: Expected number of assets exposed across all simulations
- pct_portfolio_exposed: Fraction of portfolio with exposure probability > 0
- max_assets_exposed: Worst-case scenario (highest single simulation count)

RISK LEVEL CLASSIFICATION
==========================
Exposure probability thresholds:
- Critical:  >= 80%  (8 in 10 simulations show exposure)
- High:      >= 60%
- Medium:    >= 30%
- Low:       >= 10%
- Minimal:   <  10%

HOW TO USE THIS IN PRACTICE
============================
1. Start with the Monte Carlo dashboard at /risk/monte-carlo
2. Adjust the mode_year slider based on your threat intelligence assessment
3. Use a lower sigma (1-2) if you have high confidence in timing; higher sigma
   (5-8) if deeply uncertain
4. All assets with exposure probability >= 60% should be in Phase 1 migration
5. All "At Risk" certificates require immediate PQC replacement planning
6. Report to board using P50 for central estimate, P95 for worst-case

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS
===========================
Q: What is HNDL (Harvest Now, Decrypt Later)?
A: Adversaries are collecting encrypted traffic today to decrypt once CRQC arrives.
   For data with Y >= 5 years sensitivity, HNDL exposure is already active even
   without a CRQC existing today.

Q: How is migration time (X) calculated?
A: QuShield computes dynamic migration complexity based on asset type, crypto
   agility score, third-party dependencies, certificate pinning, and forward
   secrecy support. Typical range: 1-8 years.

Q: Should we use P5 or P50 for our planning?
A: P5 for SWIFT/core banking/critical infrastructure. P50 for standard banking
   assets. P95 only for very long-lived archival data with low sensitivity.

Q: What algorithms should we migrate to?
A: NIST-standardised PQC algorithms (FIPS 203/204/205):
   - ML-KEM (CRYSTALS-Kyber): Key encapsulation
   - ML-DSA (CRYSTALS-Dilithium): Digital signatures
   - SLH-DSA (SPHINCS+): Hash-based signatures (stateless)
